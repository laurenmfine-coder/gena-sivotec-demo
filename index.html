The Problem

Physicians aren't trained to think genetically

Medical education produces clinicians who are excellent at recognizing common presentations — and systematically underprepared for the multisystem patterns that define rare genetic disease.

The result is not bad physicians. It is physicians who were never taught to ask about burning pain in the hands since adolescence, or to connect unexplained kidney disease in a brother with early cardiovascular death in a maternal uncle.

GENA identifies these patterns algorithmically. This platform teaches clinicians to recognize them humanly — so that GENA becomes a tool they trust and use, not a black box they ignore.

The Educational Model

Built around how diagnostic delay actually happens

Rare diseases are missed not because clinicians are careless — but because they were never taught to see the pattern. This platform teaches pattern recognition at the point where it matters most.

01
🔍
Same complaint, different answer

Every case family opens with an identical chief complaint. Five patients, one complaint, five different diagnoses. Learners cannot pattern-match from the opening — they must read every clinical detail carefully. This is the core reasoning skill GENA's clinical adoption depends on.

02
🧬
The constellation moment

Genetic diagnoses are rarely made from a single finding. They emerge when a clinician assembles findings across organ systems into a pattern no common disease can explain. The platform teaches exactly this — Fabry disease is in the burning feet, the absent sweating, the corneal opacity, the unexplained CKD, the LVH, and the maternal uncle who died at 46.

03
📋
GENA enters at the right moment

Every case debrief includes a GENA integration callout — explaining where in the diagnostic process GENA Screen adds value, what inputs it needs, and critically: when it is not indicated. Clinicians who understand when to use GENA are the ones who will use it correctly.
