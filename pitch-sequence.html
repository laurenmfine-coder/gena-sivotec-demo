The case for GENA
Act I
The Patient

Marcus T., 41 years old.
Six years. Three physicians.
Zero diagnosis.

Marcus is a finance professional who presented with fatigue, hypertension, and what three successive physicians called treatment-resistant depression. He was on sertraline for six months with zero improvement. Not one physician asked about the burning pain in his hands.

Yr 1
First Presentation

Treatment-resistant depression

Fatigue. Hypertension. Mood changes. Sertraline prescribed. The acroparesthesias Marcus has had since adolescence — burning pain in his hands — were never asked about.

Yr 2–3
Escalation

Second and third physician

Two more physicians. More antidepressants. An ECG showing subtle LVH attributed to hypertension. A creatinine of 1.4 attributed to aging. No one connected the findings.

Yr 4–5
The Missing Question

Family history never taken

Marcus's maternal uncle died at 46 of "kidney problems and burning hands." His mother has Stage 2 CKD attributed to "unknown cause." This information existed. No physician asked for it.

Yr 6
Diagnosis

Fabry disease — finally

Alpha-galactosidase A critically low. GLA pathogenic variant confirmed. Fabry disease. Enzyme replacement therapy initiated. His brother Raul — with unexplained CKD at 38 — tested next. Also Fabry. The diagnosis that should have taken one visit took six years.

💡
The GENA Insight
The constellation was complete at Visit 1. Fatigue + acroparesthesias + hypertension + family history of kidney disease and early death in a maternal male relative = Fabry disease until proven otherwise. GENA would have surfaced this before a single antidepressant was prescribed.
Act II
The Recognition Gap

This isn't a physician failure.
It's a system failure.

Marcus's physicians weren't negligent. They were undertrained in pattern recognition for rare disease — which is exactly what medical education produces. Two chapters of genetics in four years of medical school. No curriculum for constellation-based reasoning. No tool at the point of care.

Why physicians miss the diagnosis

01
Education gap: Medical schools train for common diagnoses. Rare disease pattern recognition is not systematically taught. There is no curriculum for "what does Fabry disease look like when it presents as fatigue?"
02
Constellation blindness: Physicians are trained to think in differential diagnoses, not in constellations. They see each finding individually. No tool connects them across time and family history.
03
No trigger for suspicion: You cannot refer for what you don't suspect. If the physician doesn't recognize the constellation, they never open a genetic tool, never order the right test, never make the diagnosis.
04
Tool gap: PharmCAT and GeneSight tell you how to dose a drug after you've made the diagnosis. No tool helps you make the diagnosis in the first place.

The same problem in common disease

01
Asthma is not one disease: Six distinct endotypes. Same chief complaint, different biology, different optimal treatments. 26 million patients receiving the same stepwise protocol regardless of inflammatory phenotype.
02
Wrong biologic, wrong patient: Six FDA-approved biologics, no head-to-head trials, no validated point-of-care selection tool. Biologic choice is often empiric, formulary-driven, or pharma-representative-driven.
03
Rare disease hiding in common presentation: EGPA presents as severe asthma. A1AT deficiency presents as childhood asthma. VCD is treated as refractory asthma for years. The same constellation logic that catches Fabry catches these.
04
Overtreatment is as costly as undertreatment: T2-low asthma patients receiving biologics that have no evidence of benefit in their phenotype. $20,000/year. Zero response. GENA prevents this as reliably as it prevents missed diagnoses.
The unifying insight

The recognition problem is the same problem stated twice — once in rare disease, once in common disease. GENA's constellation engine solves both with the same underlying logic.

GENA
Constellation analysis → phenotype identification → treatment recommendation
The physician enters the clinical constellation. GENA returns rare disease exclusion, endotype probabilities, ranked treatment recommendation, genetic testing indication, and cascade testing plan.

GENA's engine runs four simultaneous layers: (1) Rare disease exclusion — before any treatment is selected. (2) Phenotype identification — which endotype of this disease is this patient? Does the family need cascade testing? This runs across rare disease identification (Fabry, XLA, A1AT, Angelman) and common disease phenotyping (asthma endotypes, RA biologic selection, IBD endotyping) with the same underlying logic.

Act V
The Partnership Model

What if better phenotyping
changed formulary access?

The most powerful version of GENA's commercial model is a three-way alignment between pharma, payers, and GENA — where correct phenotyping is the condition for formulary placement, and everyone's incentives point in the same direction.

PHARMA
Funds disease state education (unrestricted) CME programs teaching phenotype recognition. Not branded to a specific drug. Focused on the biology — T2-high vs. T2-low, AERD constellation, EGPA thresholds. Physicians who complete the education prescribe more appropriately, not more volume.
GENA
Provides the validated phenotyping tool Algorithm is independent of pharma influence. Evidence-based. Discloses all funding relationships. Generates phenotype documentation that is product-agnostic — it recommends based on the constellation, not the formulary.
PAYERS
Require phenotype documentation for prior auth GENA documentation is accepted as the standard. Biologics approved only for documented phenotype-matched patients. Non-responders are identified before prescription, not after six months of failed therapy.
PHYSICIANS
Educated to recognize, trained to document CME creates recognition. GENA provides the tool. Prior auth creates the workflow incentive. The physician uses GENA because the science supports it and because the system requires it.
PATIENTS
Get the right treatment the first time Priya gets dupilumab + aspirin desensitization, not omalizumab. Marcus gets augmentation therapy, not a biologic that achieves nothing. Diane's EGPA is caught before permanent neuropathy. Rachel is stepped down, not escalated.
The outcome for pharma
Better phenotype matching means more responders per prescription. Real-world response rates improve. Payer coverage expands. The drug's reputation strengthens. The biologic that was being used for the wrong patients — and failing — is now being used for the right patients. GENA doesn't threaten pharma's market. It makes their market better.
The conflict of interest guardrail
Pharma funds the education. Pharma does not control the algorithm. The GENA phenotyping engine recommends based on the clinical constellation — not based on which company funded the surrounding education. That independence is disclosed, auditable, and non-negotiable. It is also what makes GENA credible to clinicians, payers, and regulators. The moment GENA is seen as a pharma tool, clinician trust collapses. Independence is not just ethical — it is commercially essential.

Which pharma partner is most aligned with GENA's beachhead disease space — and would fund an unrestricted educational grant structured to preserve GENA's clinical independence?
